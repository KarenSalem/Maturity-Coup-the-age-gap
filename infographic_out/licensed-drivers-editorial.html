FHWA DL-220 / licensed drivers by sex and age group

The age of delayed independence

The clearest story in the licensed-driver table is not just that Americans are living longer. It is that the road became less youth-heavy. In 1963, drivers age 16 through 24 made up 18.6 percent of all licensed drivers. In 2024, they made up 11.2 percent. At the same time, women moved from 39.6 percent of licensed drivers to 50.5 percent.

What the data says

A driver’s license used to be a rite of passage. It is becoming a later-life milestone.

This dataset is especially useful because it separates age and sex over six decades. That lets us see two shifts at once. First, the youth slice of the driver pool shrank. Second, the gender gap collapsed from male-heavy to parity and, in older ages, female-heavy. Those are the ingredients of a much broader story about delayed adulthood.
Editorial framing: use as a proxy for delayed independence, not as standalone proof of every social cause
“Between 1963 and 2024, the youth share of licensed drivers fell by nearly 40 percent.” The precise change is from 18.6 percent to 11.2 percent for ages 16-24. It is a compact sentence journalists can quote and readers can understand immediately.

Three lines a reporter can quote

  • Women went from 39.6 percent of licensed drivers in 1963 to 50.5 percent in 2024.
  • The 16-24 driver share fell from 18.6 percent to 11.2 percent over the same span.
  • In 2024, the first female-majority age band is 40-44, which tells you where parity begins to show up in the life course.

How to frame the story

  • Strongest claim: the road is becoming less teen-heavy and more adult-heavy.
  • Best caution: this is evidence of delayed licensing and changing life-course behavior, not a standalone proof of every cause.
  • Best use: pair this with housing, labor-force, school, and household formation data for a broader autonomy story.
Methodology
Counts are from FHWA Highway Statistics Table DL-220 via the DOT open-data portal. The source file records licensed drivers by year, sex, and age cohort, with values published in thousands. I treated blank cells as missing, not zero, and used the official 2024 CSV export as the base dataset for the visuals and summary copy.
