# Why this overlay matters

- Why we want it:
  - It lets the summer-work series and teen licensing series move together in one frame.
  - It shows the reader that youth autonomy is not only about getting a license, but also about earning and participating in summer work.

- Why it adds value:
  - The overlay connects two independent decline lines into one shared maturity-gap story.
  - It makes it easier to see that the loss of summer work and the drop in teen licensure are not separate conversations.
  - It gives the package a simple visual bridge between income, mobility, and independence.

- Why it is not just nice to have:
  - On its own, the licensure chart can feel like a transportation trend.
  - On its own, the summer-work chart can feel like a labor-force trend.
  - Together, they read as a single autonomy story, which is the core of the package.

- What a journalist would miss without it:
  - They might treat youth work and teen licensure as unrelated metrics.
  - They might miss the shared direction of change, which is what makes the “maturity gap” argument stronger.
  - They might not see that the decline in summer earnings and the decline in driving access reinforce each other.

- Where I would be careful:
  - Do not claim the overlay proves one trend caused the other.
  - Keep the framing as correlation and shared direction, not direct causation.
  - Keep the 18-year-old anchor clear, since that is the age the story is built around.

- Honest take:
  - Yes, this adds real value.
  - It is the cleanest bridge graphic in the package because it visually connects work and mobility.
  - If the main thesis is “we stopped letting them grow up,” this is one of the strongest supporting visuals.
