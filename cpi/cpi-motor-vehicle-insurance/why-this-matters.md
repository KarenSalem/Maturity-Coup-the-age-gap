# Why this CPI layer is in the package

- Why we want it:
  - It gives the story a cost side, not just a youth-participation side.
  - It helps explain why the path to independence got harder even if behavior alone does not tell the whole story.

- Why it adds value:
  - The summer-work chart shows weaker youth earning/participation.
  - The CPI insurance chart shows one of the recurring costs of independence rising much faster than general inflation.
  - Together, they show a squeeze from both directions: less income flow and higher mobility costs.

- Why it is not just nice to have:
  - It turns a single-trend narrative into a structural one.
  - It gives the package a concrete economic pressure that readers can recognize immediately.
  - It makes the maturity-gap argument feel grounded in measurable conditions, not just cultural interpretation.

- What a journalist would miss without it:
  - They might write the youth work decline as a labor-market or preference story.
  - They might write the licensure decline as a behavior or technology story.
  - They could miss the fact that driving became materially more expensive at the same time young people were earning less.

- Where I would be careful:
  - Do not present the CPI series as teen-specific insurance pricing.
  - Do not claim the insurance trend proves causation for the work or licensure decline.
  - Use the III teen-driver context as supporting explanation, not as a substitute for a direct teen premium history.

- Honest take:
  - Yes, this adds real value.
  - It is best used as a supporting layer, not the headline proof.
  - The core story is still the youth-autonomy trend; this layer helps explain why the endpoint became harder to reach.
